Why "Squawk 77"?
7700

When a pilot declares an emergency, they enter a four-digit code into a device called a transponder. That code broadcasts to every radar screen in range, telling air traffic controllers instantly that something has gone wrong.

The code is 7700. In aviation, it's called squawking the code — so a plane in distress is said to be squawking 7700. Squawk 77 for short.

This site exists for the same reason that code does: to make sure that when something goes wrong in the sky, the world pays attention. Every accident covered here was a 7700 moment — and every one of them quietly made flying safer for the rest of us.

Case 01 of 03  ·  Runway Collision

A Chain of Coincidences. 583 People Dead.

A bomb in a flower shop. A volcanic island that makes its own fog. An experienced captain who spent more time in a simulator than a cockpit. Every single link in this chain had to hold for the disaster to happen — and every single one of them did.

Date27 March 1977
LocationTenerife, Spain
Fatalities583
// KLM Flight 4805 · PH-BUF · The Rhine
// Pan Am Flight 1736 · N736PA · Clipper Victor

The Canary Islands in 1977 had a political problem. A separatist group called the MPAIAC was pushing for independence from Spain, and on the afternoon of 27 March, two of their members walked into the terminal building at Las Palmas airport on Gran Canaria, left a bag next to the flower shop near the check-in desks, and walked out. The bag was ticking. At 1pm a desk clerk received a phone call: there were bombs — plural — in the building. Fifteen minutes later the one next to the flower shop exploded. The terminal was damaged, four people were injured, and Las Palmas airport was closed while authorities searched for the rest.

Flights began diverting to Los Rodeos, the only nearby alternative. Los Rodeos sat on the northeastern corner of Tenerife, up in the hills at 2,000 feet — directly in the path of the humid Atlantic trade winds that push against the slopes of Pico de Teide, Spain's highest mountain. When those winds cool against the hillside, the moisture falls and clouds form fast. Not ordinary fog — rolling banks that could drop visibility from three kilometres to 300 metres in minutes, then lift just as quickly. The airport had no ground radar. It had a single runway with one taxiway running alongside it. It was not built for major operations.

Among the aircraft diverted there were KLM Flight 4805 from Amsterdam — 234 passengers, 14 crew — and Pan American Flight 1736 from Los Angeles, via New York — 380 passengers, 16 crew. Both were bound for Las Palmas. Both were now sitting on an apron that was filling up fast. The taxiways became so congested that there was no longer room to use them. Any aircraft wanting to take off would have to use the runway itself to taxi to the end.

KLM's passengers were let off into the terminal. Pan Am's couldn't be — the terminal was full — so they waited on board in the afternoon heat. Captain Victor Grubbs, in command of the Pan Am flight, tried to make the best of it. He invited passengers up to the flight deck to look around. The mood was reasonable, given the circumstances.

In the KLM cockpit the mood was different. Captain Jacob Veldhuyzen van Zanten was KLM's chief flight instructor on the 747 fleet — the most senior pilot in the entire operation. His face appeared in the airline's own advertising. He was 50 years old, experienced, highly decorated. He was also, by the nature of his instructor role, a man who spent more of his hours in the simulator than in actual aircraft. And he was very anxious about his duty time. New Dutch legislation had recently brought in strict limits on how long a crew could operate — breach them and a captain could face a judge. Van Zanten could see that if the delay stretched much longer, there would be no time to fly to Las Palmas, turn around, and get back to Amsterdam within the rules. He was also worried about his wife. He knew she would hear news of the bomb and assume he was in Las Palmas — and he didn't want her spending the night not knowing where he was.

So while they waited, van Zanten made a decision. He ordered his aircraft to be refuelled — fully, enough for Las Palmas and the return leg to Amsterdam. That way they could minimise their time on the ground once Las Palmas reopened. It was a logical choice. But a full fuel load would make the aircraft about fifteen percent heavier, requiring a higher takeoff speed and a longer runway run. And once fuelling a 747 begins, you don't simply stop it.

Only minutes after refuelling started, word came through: Las Palmas had reopened. Captain Grubbs immediately requested startup — and was told he couldn't go. The KLM aircraft was blocking the only exit path. The Pan Am crew sent their engineer and first officer out to physically measure whether they could squeeze past. They couldn't. Less than twenty feet short, but short. They would have to wait for KLM to finish.

One KLM passenger chose this moment not to reboard. Robina van Lanschot worked for the company that had chartered the flight. Her boyfriend lived on Tenerife. She had checked during the layover and been given permission to stay. As the other passengers filed back on, she was already in a car heading away from the airport. She did not know this would save her life.

"We are now at takeoff." — KLM First Officer Meurs, reading back a routing clearance. The tower heard it as a position report. Van Zanten heard it as confirmation to go.

At 16:56 the KLM crew finished starting engines and called for taxi. They were cleared to backtrack Runway 12 to its far end, make a 180-degree turn, and hold. The route clearance they had been given included the word "takeoff" — as in "right turn after takeoff, proceed on heading 040." In the KLM cockpit, van Zanten heard that word and pushed the throttles forward. First Officer Meurs stopped him. "We haven't got clearance." Van Zanten pulled back. "I know that. Go ahead, ask."

Meanwhile the Pan Am aircraft had been given the same runway to taxi down, cleared to exit at the third turnoff on the left. The third exit required a 148-degree turn — sharp enough for a small aircraft, essentially impossible for a 747 at low speed in fog. The Pan Am crew discussed it among themselves, suspected the controller meant the fourth exit, and kept taxiing slowly in zero visibility looking for it. They missed the third exit entirely, and nobody on the frequency caught it. The two aircraft were now facing each other, unseen, somewhere on the same strip of runway.

The approach controller, also trying to issue KLM's routing clearance, used the wrong call sign. The clearance contained the word "takeoff." Meurs read it back, stressed, ending with "we are now at takeoff" — intended to convey their position. Van Zanten, who had already started rolling once, heard it as permission. The tower replied: "OK — stand by for takeoff, I will call you." At that exact second, the Pan Am crew broke in on the same frequency: "We're still taxiing down the runway." The two transmissions collided and produced a burst of static. In the KLM cockpit, the crew heard one word: "OK." Then nothing. Van Zanten pushed the throttles to full power.

Flight engineer Wilhelm Schreiber had been listening to the radio. He leaned forward: "Is he not clear of the runway, that Pan Am?" Van Zanten, eyes on the runway ahead, barely registering the question, said "Oh yes" — and kept the throttles forward. Those were the last three moments that could have stopped it. The controller thought he had told KLM to stand by. Pan Am thought they had warned everyone. The flight engineer had raised the question and been dismissed. The runway was dark, the fog absolute.

Captain Grubbs saw the KLM's landing lights appear out of the fog eleven seconds before impact. "Oh God damn it — that son of a bitch is coming." First Officer Bragg: "Get off, get off." Grubbs threw full power and full left tiller, trying to drag 340 tons of aircraft into the grass. They reached twenty knots. Van Zanten, crossing V1, saw the Pan Am silhouette turning in front of him. He hauled back on the yoke — a desperate attempt to climb over it — causing a tail strike that left twenty metres of metal scrapings on the runway. The KLM's number four engine, on the right wing, sliced into the Pan Am's first-class lounge. The main gear tore through the fuselage. The left wing ripped away the Pan Am's tail. The KLM flew on for another 150 metres, then sank back onto the runway without its landing gear, and exploded.

Of the 396 people on the Pan Am aircraft, 61 survived. Most escaped through holes torn in the fuselage. The emergency exits were unusable — the impact had buckled the entire structure. Survivors jumped. The drop to the asphalt was nearly six metres. Every single person on the KLM aircraft died. The fires burned for hours. 583 people in total were killed.

The air traffic controller had no idea it had happened. The airport had no ground radar. The visibility was too poor to see from the tower. It was another aircraft, parked on a taxiway, that reported seeing flames on the runway. The fire crews drove out slowly through the fog — carefully, so they wouldn't run over anyone in the dark. They came first to the outline of a 747 fully engulfed in flames. Then, further down, they found another one.

The Spanish investigation blamed van Zanten: taking off without clearance, not stopping when told to stand by, not stopping when Pan Am called, not stopping when his own engineer asked. The Dutch investigation dug deeper, asking not just what happened but why an experienced captain made each of those decisions. That question produced a new framework for understanding cockpit culture — and it led directly to rules that are still in force today.

The word "takeoff" was removed from all communications except the actual clearance to take off. Pilots now say "departure" or "rolling" in every other context. "OK" was banned from critical readbacks entirely. Reading back every clearance in full became mandatory worldwide. Taxiway markings were redesigned to be legible in low visibility. Ground radar was mandated at airports handling significant traffic.

And the question of cockpit authority was confronted directly. First Officer Meurs had 9,200 hours — he was not an inexperienced co-pilot afraid of his captain. He did push back, twice. But van Zanten overrode him both times with total certainty. The Dutch investigators concluded that the psychological pressure of duty-time limits, combined with van Zanten's extraordinary seniority, had created a situation where doubt was expressed but never acted on. CRM — the principle that any crew member can and must stop a captain making a fatal error — became mandatory training across the industry. Tenerife is where it was born.

Case 02 of 03  ·  Structural Failure

12,319 Flights. Then the Bulkhead Let Go.

A botched repair in 1978 introduced a flaw that no inspection ever caught. For seven years it grew quietly with every flight. On 12 August 1985, it reached its limit — and three pilots were left to fly a 747 with no tail, no hydraulics, and 509 passengers, using nothing but engine thrust.

Date12 Aug 1985
LocationJapanese Alps
Fatalities520
// JA8119 · Tokyo Haneda Airport · The aircraft involved in Flight 123

It was the beginning of Obon — the Japanese holiday season when millions travel home to their families. Flight 123, Tokyo to Osaka, was one of the busiest routes in the country. On this evening it was full: 509 passengers and 15 crew on a 747SR, a short-range variant Japan Airlines had commissioned specifically for high-frequency domestic operations. The SR had a reinforced airframe built for the punishment of multiple pressurisation cycles a day, reduced fuel capacity, and more seats than any other aircraft type then in service. It was the workhorse of the domestic network.

In command was 49-year-old Captain Masami Takahama, with over 12,000 hours and more than a decade on the 747. Beside him, acting as captain under supervision on an upgrade check, was 39-year-old First Officer Yutaka Sasaki, with nearly 4,000 hours. At the flight engineer's station sat 46-year-old Hiroshi Fukuda — almost 10,000 hours total, 4,000 of them on this type. This was his third sector of the day on this aircraft. An experienced crew. They had handled everything the 747 had thrown at them before. They had not been trained for what was about to happen.

// Inside the cabin during the emergency · Photograph taken by a passenger

Seven years earlier, this aircraft had made a hard landing at Osaka and struck its tail on the runway. The rear pressure bulkhead — an umbrella-shaped curved wall that sealed the pressurised cabin from the unpressurised tail section — was badly damaged. Boeing engineers came and carried out the repair. They made one mistake: instead of using a single continuous splice plate to bridge the join between the upper and lower bulkhead sections, they cut the plate in two. This meant the top rivet row was no longer carrying its share of the load. With every subsequent flight, as the cabin pressurised and depressurised, the stress concentrated in the wrong place. Tiny cracks formed around the overloaded rivets. They grew. No routine inspection found them.

By 12 August 1985 the aircraft had completed 12,319 flights since the repair. Twelve minutes after departing Tokyo, climbing over Tokyo Bay on a summer evening, the bulkhead finally gave way.

"Something has exploded! Squawk 77." — JAL 123 crew, 18:24 local time, setting the emergency transponder code as the tail tore away.

The pressurised air inside the cabin blasted through the hole in an instant. The wave of pressure ripped the entire vertical tail fin off the aircraft, along with the rudder, the auxiliary power unit, and several other critical components. Everything in the rear cabin that wasn't secured was flung backwards. Ceiling panels were torn away. Oxygen masks dropped throughout the aircraft. Flight attendants scrambled to help passengers fit them while taking turns breathing from the few spare masks hanging from the ceiling.

In the cockpit the cabin altitude alarm was screaming. Captain Takahama set the transponder to 7700 — the universal distress squawk — and instructed Sasaki to turn back toward Tokyo. The plane banked right and kept banking. No matter what Sasaki did with the controls, he couldn't stop it. The flight engineer watched the hydraulic gauges drain to zero. All four systems — every one of them — had been severed by shrapnel in the same section where they passed close together near the tail. The controls were gone. The rudder was gone. The pilots had become passengers.

Without the tail fin to stabilise it, the aircraft began swinging from side to side in a phenomenon called Dutch roll — like a falling leaf, nauseating and uncontrollable. Without hydraulics to move the elevators, it entered phugoid motion: climbing until it lost speed, then pitching forward and diving until it regained it, then climbing again. The crew were cycling through thousands of feet with every oscillation, unable to stop it. Tokyo ATC called. The pilots didn't answer. They were too overloaded — and at 24,000 feet, where the air contains barely a third of the oxygen available at ground level, hypoxia was quietly setting in. The brain's cortex — responsible for complex problem-solving, exactly what they needed — is the first area affected by oxygen deprivation. The crew's thinking was becoming foggy without them knowing it.

They hadn't put their oxygen masks on. They were so focused on the controls that the thought simply didn't occur to them. The flight engineer, slightly less overloaded, tried to reach the captain and get him to start an emergency descent. The captain agreed — then carried on as before, unable in his hypoxic state to convert the decision into action.

There was one thing left to work with: the engines. By carefully timing changes to thrust — reducing power as the nose pitched up, adding it as the nose came down — the crew could partially dampen the phugoid cycle. They worked at it. Against every odd, they made some progress. The ride became slightly less violent. The flight engineer suggested lowering the landing gear — unorthodox, but it would add drag and slow the aircraft, helping it descend. On the 747, as on most jets, the gear can drop under its own weight without hydraulic power. They tried it. The gear locked down. The phugoid improved somewhat — but the Dutch roll worsened. Smoothing one axis meant losing the other. They couldn't fix both at once.

On the right side of the cabin, passengers could see Mount Fuji drifting past beneath them. Beyond it, the sprawl of Tokyo. Impossibly close. ATC controllers watching the radar could see the aircraft oscillating wildly, climbing and diving through thousands of feet, over the mountains northwest of the city. They could do nothing. Japan Airlines had been notified. They couldn't do anything either.

As the aircraft descended below 20,000 feet the oxygen density began to rise. Slowly, the pilots' minds cleared. For the first time since the explosion they could think rather than just react. They contacted Tokyo approach and asked for their position: 55 miles northwest of Haneda. Not impossible. If they could turn around and maintain this altitude, they might make it back.

The first officer managed, using asymmetric thrust, to begin a right turn back toward the city. Then he tried to extend the flaps for stability. The hydraulic system was gone, but the backup electrical system could move them — slowly. He got partial extension. For a brief moment the aircraft flew level. The crew sensed a chance. Then the flaps kept moving, past their commanded position, and the left wing's flaps extended further than the right. The asymmetry lifted the left wing. The plane began rolling right. The first officer pushed the left throttles harder than the right — neither he nor anyone else noticed. The bank steepened. At nine thousand feet, with mountains filling the windscreen, the right wingtip and its engine struck a ridge. The plane spun onto its back and hit the mountainside at several hundred kilometres per hour. It was 18:56, thirty-two minutes after the bulkhead failed.

Of the 524 people on board, four survived. All were seated in the rear of the aircraft. A US Air Force aircraft flying nearby offered to help locate survivors — and was turned down. Japanese authorities, believing no one could have survived, did not dispatch rescue teams until dawn. It was nearly fourteen hours before anyone reached the site. Doctors who examined the survivors later determined that some of those who died in the hours after the crash might have lived had rescuers arrived sooner. The delayed response was widely condemned and directly changed how rescue operations in Japan are conducted — teams are now dispatched immediately regardless of whether survival seems possible.

Boeing redesigned the 747's tail structure so that a rapid pressure spike in the tailplane would no longer cause the kind of explosive structural failure that destroyed Flight 123. They also redesigned the hydraulic systems, installing a fuse mechanism so that losing the tail fin would not automatically drain all four hydraulic lines. Japan Airlines overhauled its maintenance procedures, introducing independent verification of all major structural repairs. The accident remains the deadliest single-aircraft disaster in aviation history.

The splice plate error that started it all was one cut in the wrong place on one repair, signed off by engineers who followed the wrong procedure. It passed 12,319 inspections across seven years. Flight 123 is the reason structural repair records are now scrutinised the way they are — and the reason independent sign-off on major repairs is mandatory everywhere in the world.

Case 03 of 03  ·  Crew Resource Management

The Crash Nobody Should Have Walked Away From

When a fan disc shattered inside the tail engine of United 232, it didn't just destroy that engine — it severed all three hydraulic lines at once. A failure engineers said had a one in a billion chance of happening. There was no checklist for it. No procedure. Just four men and two throttles.

Date19 Jul 1989
LocationSioux City, Iowa
Fatalities111
// N1819U · Newark International Airport · 1980 · The aircraft involved in Flight 232

It was a clear July afternoon over Iowa. United Flight 232 had been airborne for nearly an hour out of Denver, on autopilot, heading for Chicago. Captain Alfred Haynes, Copilot William Records, and Flight Engineer Dudley Dvorak — two experienced pilots and an engineer on a routine flight. Then a violent bang shook the rear of the aircraft.

Dvorak reported the tail engine had failed. Haynes started the shutdown checklist. A lost engine on a DC-10 is serious but survivable — the aircraft is designed to fly on two. But when Records reached for the controls, nothing responded. Haynes tried himself. Nothing. Dvorak checked the hydraulic gauges. All three systems were reading zero. Every drop of fluid was gone.

The DC-10 carries three completely independent hydraulic systems for exactly this reason — so no single failure can take all three out. They run separately through the aircraft, but near the tail there is one point where they pass close together, beside the rear engine. Inside that engine, a fan disc had been slowly cracking for years — a microscopic impurity in the titanium that had passed every inspection undetected. When it finally shattered, fragments tore through all three lines simultaneously. There was no procedure for this. No one had written one because the odds were considered one in a billion.

// The recovered Stage 1 fan disc · NTSB evidence photograph
"The loss of all hydraulic fluid was considered virtually impossible. No emergency checklist existed for it." — NTSB accident report, 1990.

Without hydraulics the plane began banking right, toward an unrecoverable roll. Haynes realised the only remaining tool was the engines themselves. By pulling back on the left throttle he could fight the drift and level the aircraft. It wasn't flying — it was managing a slow-motion fall.

A flight attendant came to the cockpit for an update, then returned to the cabin. She was stopped by a calm passenger in the front row of first class: Dennis Fitch, a United Airlines DC-10 flight instructor. When she told him the rear engine had shut down and all hydraulics were lost, he understood immediately what that meant. He had studied Japan Airlines Flight 123, four years earlier, where the identical failure had killed 520 people. He walked to the cockpit and offered to help. Haynes let him in without hesitation.

While Haynes and Records handled navigation and communication with ATC, Fitch stood between the two pilots' seats with a throttle in each hand — increasing and decreasing engine power on Haynes's commands, steering the aircraft by feel. Chicago was unreachable. ATC identified Sioux City airport, under 100 miles away. The plane could only reliably turn right, so they corkscrewed toward it in wide loops, dumping fuel as they went.

There were 52 children on board. It was Children's Day — a United promotion where children flew for a penny. Many were travelling alone. Flight attendants moved through the cabin, pairing every child with an adult. For the four infants too young for a seat, there was nothing to do except lay them on the floor and surround them with pillows.

On final approach Haynes realised they weren't lined up with the active runway. But they were almost perfectly aligned with Runway 22 — closed permanently the year before. Emergency crews scrambled to clear it. Just above the ground, the plane lurched right. The wingtip struck first. The aircraft cartwheeled, broke apart, and ignited. The wreckage slid down the closed runway and into the cornfields beyond.

Passengers walked out of the fire. One man escaped, heard a baby crying inside the wreckage, and went back in — finding an infant thrown into an overhead bin during the impact. He carried her out and handed her to her mother. Forty minutes later, rescuers spotted Flight Engineer Dvorak waving from the shattered cockpit. All four crew were alive. Of the 296 people on board, 185 survived. Every simulation run afterwards showed that any conventional response — anything by the book — would have saved no one.

United 232 is the defining case study for Crew Resource Management. Four people — a captain, a first officer, a flight engineer, and an instructor who happened to be a passenger — divided the work, communicated constantly, and kept each other honest for 44 minutes. None of them could have done it alone. The crash made CRM mandatory in cockpits worldwide. Before Tenerife and United 232, aviation cockpit culture was built on hierarchy and silence. After them, it became one of shared decision-making and open communication. That shift has saved countless lives in the decades since.
